Texas Instruments

For much of summer 2019, I stayed in Sugar Land, Texas to work as a Software Engineering Intern with Texas Instruments on the C7x Compiler Team (Codegen). I worked on a project to increase performance on C7x device software, so I learned a great deal about software pipelining, SIMD (Single Instruction Multiple Data) and VLIW registers, as well as how compilers work.

In Spring 2018, I enjoyed learning about computer architecture so much that I decided to embark on Texas A&M University's Embedded Systems Integration Minor in Fall 2018 with ESET classes. Since Texas Instruments has such close ties with Texas A&M University, I was able to quickly start learning how to use the hardware and software capabilities of a new architecture, which prepared me for the learning curve at TI. I consider myself lucky to get to work at the home of the DSP, and I enjoy learning about the advances in embedded processing and analog technology in its past, present, and future. On Codegen, I had a much more deeply technical role than I had at Lockheed Martin. Since much of the DSP industry that uses TI products (such as cellular infrastructure and communications equipment) uses the C6000 product line, much of the software written for C6x products heavily utilizes intrinsics, as was advertised (see "intrinsics" section on page 8). Now that TI's new C7000 archtecture makes heavy use of these legacy intrinsics, we can utilize the VLIW registers that are 8x wider to widen the software pipeline to drastically improve performance when migrating C6x code to C7x. For the 10 weeks I worked for TI this summer, I read/wrote lots of C code from the parser to the instruction matcher, as well as learned a few languages internal to the compiler.

Some of my Biggest Takeaways:

  • Look for Patterns and Break Problems Down

    One of the most important observations I made when working with these intrinsics was that C6x code (within intrinsics or in user code) tends to treat data a certain way. C6x traditionally takes packed data in a 64-bit register, unpacks the data, does something with the data, and packs the result back up. Once I noticed this, I was able to look at all these 200+ intrinsics and break them down according to which of these three stages they applied to the data in a loop. This gave me a way to look for code in the optimizer common to all these three categories of data manipulation that I could optimize. Sure enough, I got great overall performance hits from each subproject.

  • What I learn in school really matters

    To complete this project, I had to understand the C language and how compilers parse source code and optimize the abstract syntax tree. Thankfully, I learned how compilers work from my programming languages class, and my MCU classes taught me about the computer architecture concept of SIMD and vector registers. Additionally, many of my teammates hold graduate degrees in the field of computer science, several of whom wrote their thesis on the work they did at TI.

  • Leadership and Technical Skill are Equally Important

    TI has both a Technical and Management ladder for promoting employees who demonstrate technical or menagerial leadership to their peers. I met many senior members who taught me that TI values individual contributions to both leading a team and contributing deep technical knowledge.

  • Practice Presenting Technically is Always Helpful

    One thing I identified from my final presentation that I could do better in my career is presenting technically to a diverse audience. I had difficulty targeting my presentation to both the technically savvy and the first time programmers, and I figured that if I practice at events like hackathons and in class projects during my last year at Texas A&M University, I could get much better at it before I start working full time.

  • Agile is Different in Practice from the Theory

    Throughout this internship, I practiced Agile through TI's Jira system. I filed stories and bugs as they came up and as I broke my project down into pieces, and reported on my project each day. Every iteration (every 2 weeks), my team got together to share what got done on that iteration and what is coming up in the next one. During these meetings, we analyze our long-term progress and check on the backlog status.

  • Failing Fast and Asking Questions Soon Really Does Help

    Towards the beginning of my internship, I tried to solve too many problems by myself and spent too much time struggling with one blocking issue. I learned that asking questions, no matter how stupid they may seem, could lead to insights about something no one has ever seen. Some of my best learning opportunities were when I caused a nighly validation to fail.
