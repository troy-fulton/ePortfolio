Coursework

Below I have listed the courses I have taken at Texas A&M so far. I have gone into detail about my experiences in classes from which I have learned a lot about the world. There are some classes, like Anthropology, that I was cautious to take because they made me uncomfortable, but I have learned that it is from those classes that I grow the most. Hopefully, in the future, I will get the chance to take more classes like these and continue to positively change my perspective on areas of study inside and out of my major.

Freshman Year (Fall 2016 and Spring 2017)

  • *Texas A&M First-Year Engineering Program (ENGR 111/112): These classes challenged me to think algorithmically and coordinate with engineers of unfamiliar disciplines in order to assemble robotics projects and presentations throughout the year. I also learned my first coding language, MATLAB, as I worked both on rigorous individual assignments and group coding projects. My professors and peers enculturated me to think like an engineer and thirst for ways to both understand the universe and use my findings to improve lives around the world.
  • *Design Process (ENDS 101): The knowledge I gained from this class, such as how to file a provisionary patent, was invaluable. In this course, I experienced what it is like to work on interdisciplinary projects with people of mindsets completely different from my own. My main takeaway from this course was that I learned to create knowledge rather than reproduce it.
  • *University Honors Seminar (UGST 181):In these "family meetings," I met weekly with students of many other studies to discuss aspects of our college career such as career goals and our plans for our capstone projects.
  • *Introduction to Anthropology (ANTH 201): This course opened my eyes to brand new ways of thinking about our race that I don't think I would have imagined before. Witnessing the evidence of our evolution and truly trying to pin down a definition for "culture" taught me that it is in human nature to depend on one another for survival. Therefore, we have become experts at communication. The birth and history of the human race and our abilities interests me enough to want to study it more in the future, perhaps with a link to computer science that might show me a way to better develop computer languages that are easier to understand and use.
  • Linear Algebra (MATH 304)
  • Calculus III for Engineers (MATH 251)
  • Differential Equations for Engineers (MATH 308)
  • Chemistry I for Engineers (CHEM 107/117)

Sophomore Year (Fall 2017 and Spring 2018)

  • *Introduction to Program Design Concepts (CSCE 121): In this class, I learned the basics of how to organize a program such that it is easier for a reader to understand. I learned the basic syntax of C++ so that I could work on programming assignments on my own and in teams. I leared about Object Oriented Programming, as well as how to use libraries and create header file interfaces. I also learned some of the basics for other classes, like data structures, computer organization, and programming languages, through assignments that required us to construct linked lists and grammars and understand how the CPU interacts with the rest of the computer.
  • *Discrete Structures of Computing (CSCE 222): In this discrete-math-like course, I learned the basic mathematical building blocks used for many things in computer science, such as proving the efficiency of a program. In this class, we dissected topics like time complexity and recursion as well as designing finite state automata and Turing Machines.
  • Intro to Computing Seminar (CSCE 181)
  • Weather and Climate (ATMO 201)
  • Principles of Statistics I (STAT 211)
  • Computer Organization (CSCE 312): I am learning how all the parts of a CPU work together and how to program them in x86-64 assembly, as well as Y86, a "toy" version I am using for my final project. I learned how to design an ALU and register files using logic gates in Logisim, as well as data path and control elements for designing a CPU. Our labs involve analyzing C code and writing it in assembly code, and I will also soon learn about how to write cache-friendly programs.
  • Programming Languages (CSCE 314): Through the use of one functional programming language (Haskell) and one object- oriented language (Java), I have learned how to design grammars and data structures in both types of languages. With Haskell, we have written a parser for our own small language, as well as an interpreter. In Java, we have learned how to program generically in an object-oriented language, as well as the basics of Type Theory. We will soon learn the basics for concurrent programming.
  • *Data Structures and Algorithms (CSCE 221): In this class, I have learned what is at the heart of computer science. We have learned and practiced the use of abstract data types such as lists, queues, stacks, heaps, binary trees, (2,3) trees, and many more. Through programming in C++, we have implemented different data structures for each of the ADTs to see which ones are the most time and space efficient. I have also learned how to assess programs for efficiency through profiling and time complexity analysis through Visual Studio Projects. We have begun learning how to use data structures to implement different sorting algorithms and will soon learn more useful algorithms.
  • Public Speaking (COMM 203): Speaking extemporaneously in this class has taught me the importance of being able to speak to a large number of people in four specific ways. I can give an introductory speech about myself or someone else; I can speak with a group in a symposium format; I can speak persuasively on topics of policy; I can give a speech of refutation. I learned not only how to present myself to an audience but also how to best prepare for a speech by organizing my thoughts and research beforehand.
