Coursework

Undergraduate at Texas A&M University (2016-2020)

Here are the courses I took as an undergraduate at Texas A&M University. I have written about some of the most valuable takeaways from the maroon classes (expand to read more).

tl;dr: I know... it's a lot. To see classes that I believe demonstrate breadth in my coursework, see ENDS 101, ANTH 201, ATMO 201, ESET 219, and MATH 411. Some classes that demonstrate depth in my knowledge include CSCE 315, CSCE 463, CSCE 451, and ESET 349.

Freshman Year (Fall 2016 and Spring 2017)

  • *Texas A&M University First-Year Engineering Program (ENGR 111/112)

    These classes were my first glimpse at how engineers use their skills to collaboratively solve problems both large and small in the world. I learned to "think algorithmically" when facing problems that I can't solve with traditional methods I learned from mathematics and science. I learned the value of practicing good teaming strategies from projects where we designed, built, and programmed Lego EV3 Mindstorm robots in MATLAB and LabVIEW to perform tasks like sorting marbles and traversing a table.

    Texas A&M University's freshman engineering program is unique- my peers and I started school as "General Engineering" students, then applied to our disciplines once we had finished these classes. This allowed me to investigate my interests more easily than if I had been busy with basics classes in my major. During my second semester, I had my "falling in love with coding" moment and decided that the best way for me to make my positive impact on society was to continue pursuing the discipline.

    The professor I had in my second semester (ENGR 112) particularly challenged me by giving the class extra challenge problems to complete as additional homework. He held a competition throughout the semester to see who could come up with the "fastest" solution (measured by varying metrics), and by the end of the semester, I had come out with the highest number of points.

  • *Design Process (ENDS 101)

    I gained invaluable knowledge from this class, such as how to file a provisionary patent and tips on how to come up with new ideas. In this class, I was challenged to work on interdisciplinary projects with people of mindsets completely different from my own. My main takeaway from this class was the knowledge of the difference between creating knowledge and reproducing it.

    This comes in handy with my research, as I search for projects that can really contribute to the field. I'll also need to understand these concepts in case I come up with new ideas as I am working in the industry that I may want to patent or copyright. I have learned that the computer science industry depends heavily on broadly and vaguely worded patents, but a copyright on software can go much further when looking at a company's PI.

  • *University Honors Seminar (UGST 181)
    In these "family meetings," I met weekly with students of many other studies to discuss aspects of our college career such as career goals and our plans for our capstone projects. I learned the power of knowledge and the responsibility of experts to be professionals by interacting with experienced professionals and writing weekly reflections.
  • *Introduction to Anthropology (ANTH 201)

    This course opened my eyes to brand new ways of thinking about the human race that I don't think I would have imagined before. Witnessing the evidence of our evolution and truly trying to pin down a definition for "culture" taught me that it is in human nature to depend on one another for survival. Therefore, we have become experts at communication. The birth and history of the human race and our abilities interests me enough to want to study it more in the future, perhaps with a link to computer science that might show me a way to better develop computer languages that are easier to understand and use. This class has motivated me to learn more about the code of the quipu as I prepare to travel to Peru because I want to understand why it is so woven into their culture. Pun intended.

Sophomore Year (Fall 2017 and Spring 2018)

  • *Introduction to Program Design Concepts (CSCE 121)
    In this class, I learned the basics of how to design a program such that it satisfies readability, efficiency, organization, and much more. I learned the basic syntax of C++ (the first programming language I learned through the Computer Science department) to work on programming assignments on my own and in teams. I leared about Object Oriented Programming, as well as how to use libraries and create header file interfaces. I also learned some of the basics for other classes, like data structures, computer organization, and programming languages, through assignments that required us to construct linked lists and grammars and understand how the CPU interacts with the rest of the computer. I still use much of what I learned about the basic thought processes behind writing a good program in my classes.
  • *Discrete Structures of Computing (CSCE 222)

    Discrete mathematics forms a foundation for much of the theory behind the study of program complexity and optimization. This class taught me the basics of how to analyze the complexity of a program as a series/summation, how to operate on mathematical sets and functions, how to write proofs about recursion and induction, how to write a proper relation, and how to analyze formal languages. Finally, in the last part of the course, I learned the theory behind Turing Machines and how to use finite state automata to solve problems.

    I plan to use this course to satisfy 3 hours of breadth for University Honors because the foundations of our study of computing structures comes from discrete mathematics. My justification:

    The University Core Curriculum states that "Courses in this category focus on quantitative literacy in logic, patterns, and relationships." In CSCE 222, we analyzed mathematical relationships in mathematical relations and functions. We learned to recognize patterns in programs when studying program complexity, as we need to be able to quickly tell how a program behaves as the size of the program gets very large. Finally, we studied logic in the beginning of the course when we studied propositional and prepositional logic and how to write inductive proofs. The Core Curriculum also says that math courses should focus on "critical thinking, communication, and empirical and quantitative" skills. In CSCE 222, we focused on critical thinking in every aspect of the class, as the professor commonly gave us homework that challenged us to go beyond the basic concepts studied in class, and exams gave us the challenge of thinking of problems in new ways. We learned communication skills via discussions in class about how to solve problems that apply theory discussed in class. Also, we learned empirical and quantitative skills by applying our knowledge of program complexity to real programs that we might write for industry applications. We also went beyond learning just how to write a finite state machine to learn how to frame real-world problems using states and transitions. We learned that sometimes problems cannot be solved using Turing Machines, which gave us further insight into how we take a real world problem and prove whether it can be solved using the traditional computer model and communicate the problem more clearly to others.

  • Computer Organization (CSCE 312)

    This class gave me the motivation to study Computer Architecture as my project for the Undergraduate Research Scholars Program my senior year that would also serve as my senior design. It also motivated my study of Embedded Systems in my minor. Much of what I learned in my minor was taught in this class.

    I began by learning how most CPU architectures are set up (the organization of the CPU, main memory, and drivers along a bus in x86). As much of the course was centered around the x86 architecture, I learned how to write assembly programs using Y86, a "toy" simulator I use for verifying register and memory contents before and after execution. We began the course learning digital logic basics and built upon black box diagrams to form adders, shifters, register files, and more. Our group term project was to develop a small CPU in Logisim, write our own small ISA, and write a program to perform matrix multiplication. I also learned about how set associative and direct caching works and how to pipeline software and hardware by adding registers between stages such as IF, ID, EX, MEM, WB. This was my first experience writing low-level, bit-manipulative C code.

  • Programming Languages (CSCE 314)
    Through the use of one functional programming language (Haskell) and one object- oriented language (Java), I have learned how to design grammars and data structures in both types of languages. With Haskell, we have written a parser for our own small language, as well as an interpreter. In Java, we have learned how to program generically in an object-oriented language, as well as the basics of Type Theory. We will soon learn the basics for concurrent programming.
  • *Data Structures and Algorithms (CSCE 221)
    In this class, I have learned what is at the heart of computer science. We have learned and practiced the use of abstract data types such as lists, queues, stacks, heaps, binary trees, (2,3) trees, and many more. Through programming in C++, we have implemented different data structures for each of the ADTs to see which ones are the most time and space efficient. I have also learned how to assess programs for efficiency through profiling and time complexity analysis through Visual Studio Projects. We have begun learning how to use data structures to implement different sorting algorithms and will soon learn more useful algorithms.
  • Public Speaking (COMM 203)
    Speaking extemporaneously in this class has taught me the importance of being able to speak to a large number of people in four specific ways. I can give an introductory speech about myself or someone else; I can speak with a group in a symposium format; I can speak persuasively on topics of policy; I can give a speech of refutation. I learned not only how to present myself to an audience but also how to best prepare for a speech by organizing my thoughts and research beforehand.
  • Weather and Climate (ATMO 201)
    Many of the questions I have had since I was a kid about why the sky turns certain colors or how to read the maps on the weather channel were answered in this class. I learned how to interpret the forecast to make basic predictions about which areas will experience high and low temperature and pressure and what kind of precipitation to expect (we looked at a storms in and out of this class). Now I can't help but notice new things about the clouds when I look up!

Junior Year (Fall 2018 and Spring 2019)

  • Intro to Computer Systems (CSCE 313)

    I learned how to define an operating system and why we need them. This class taught me the importance of protecting hardware from malicious or unintentionally buggy code that might try to harm computer systems. I also learned how I can request resources from the operating systems via system calls and what happens when I run my code on Linux. I gained a deeper understanding of how not only the compiler and other programs work but also how the linker and loader work together when running a program. I also learned how to write concurrent and synchronous programs that utilize mutliple threads and/or processes by learning what resources they share and how to command the OS to designate those resources, such as pipelining or message sending.

    This class served as a great way to set up for the rest of my undergraduate coursework, since I needed to know what resources I could use, for example, for network processes in CSCE 463 (Networks and Distributed Processes). I did a programming assignment meant to emulate a fully functional shell as well as one meant to use interprocess communication and semaphores.

Since those kinds of projects demand team cooperation, we need to be prepared to work in teams to produce software using not only version control software such as git, but also using modern Agile methodologies for keeping up with team development progress. That is why, for each of the last three projects, we committed to SCRUM meetings and design documents, to emulate the real world. For the rest of the projects, I was put into three different groups, where we implemented a DBMS parser and interpreter for our own relational database system.   • Digital Electronics (ESET 219)

    As my first introduction to the classes in this department, I learned quickly that teamwork is key. Much of what I learned in this class overlapped with what I had learned in CSCE 312 (Computer Organization) about combinatorial and sequential logic at the gate level and MSI devices. I learned to solder my own circuit board that used IR sensors and LEDs to indicate if my robot hovered over a wire. I also learned how to design IC's to integrate into machines such as a railroad crossing light. As a mostly (unofficially) writing-intensive course, I also learned how to produce a well-documented, clear, and concise lab report. I also learned how to use professional software, such as Multisim, to program my robot using the Xilinx interface.

    My teammate and I designed the robot's electronics from the state diagram to the wiring diagram to the logic implementation to the Multisim module to the final robot.

  • Embedded Systems Development in C (ESET 269)
    My first experience programming a microcontroller other than Arduino. in this class, I learned to use Code Composer Studio to connect to a microcontroller and access its internals and components. Since I already knew the C language, I was mostly learning how to look at a wiring diagram of components to configure them in my program, such as configuring the direction of ports, as well as enabling digital to analog conversion and the other way around. I also learned how to navigate documents such as the user's guide and datasheet to get what I need to know about a microcontroller, especially those from TI.
  • *Networks and Distributed Processes (CSCE 463)

    In this fascinating class, I developed the skillset to understand and implement many protocols governing the top four layers of the protocol stack, including congestion control, flow control, and TCP connections. I implemented a multithreaded web client, DNS resolver, and reliable data transfer. For each of these projects, I was challenged to understand not only how networks are set up, but also why companies may want to implement certain parts themselves, such as an intranet.

    All development for the class was done in Visual Studio, and we heavily used the Winsock library as well as relevant system calls, such as gethostbyname(). In class, we learned about HTTP, DNS basics and vulnerabilities, and P2P at the application layer. We learned multiplexing, rdt, fairness, flow control, and congestion control at the transport layer. At the network layer, we learned different switching and routing algorithms, as well as IP and BGP and mulicasting/broadcasting. Finally, at the data-link layer, we learned CSMA, error detection (Hamming codes), and Ethernet. These lists are not at all comprehensive, as we went through much of the classic Top-Down Approach by Kurose and Ross.

  • *Software Reverse Engineering (CSCE 451)

    Although I ended up Q-dropping this class, I enjoyed learning about how to take a binary and discover the hidden meaning behind the assembly. Now that assembly can easily be acquired from the binary executable file in just about any operating system, the main struggle in this field is automated reverse compilation, in which the assembly is interpreted to reassemble to source code.

    Although it doesn't count for the minor, this class motivated me to pursue interdisciplinary cybersecurity using my ESET knowledge, which is the reason I decided to take the network security class CSCE 465. I learned how to use IDA Pro fairly proficiently and participated in the TAMU CTF competition, which I reported for this class here.

    This class gave me fairly good familiarity with x86 and the format of PE files. In this class, we also completed modules that taught us how to reverse a binary file with given format as an exe or dll file.

    The only part of the class I did not complete was the final project, through which I was to research the memory heap implemented in Windows XP and report how I could recognize patterns in programs that wish to corrupt the heap. I did research on a few basic attack mechanisms that can be seen here.

  • Math Probability (MATH 411)

    In Hennessey and Patterson's famous Computer Organization and Design book, they state that one of the eight great ideas in computers has been to make the common case fast. Much of computer science hinges on making a probabilistic guess at how data in a computer might behave, such as probabilistic data structures or predicting network usage.

    Having learned this in some of my basic computer science classes, I thought it would benefit me to sit in a class with mostly engineers of other majors and learn how to take probabilistic approaches to more general problems so that I could more easily apply what I learned to, for example, my research. The biggest takeaway from this class is that I learned to look at a situation and somewhat accurately (given certain assumptions) state the probability of certain events happening. I also had the benefit of learning about communication classes and the probabilities associated with their graphs.

  • Microcontroller Architecture (ESET 349)

    This class also overlapped a lot with my computer organization course (CSCE 312), but I had the benefit of learning new architectures, such as MIPS and ARM. I learned how to program an FPGA board to make electronic components just as easily (if not more easily) as I created them with digital logic gates. In this class, I got to read the work of Hennessey and Patterson about computer organization, whom I did a research project on in CSCE 221.

    One major thing I learned in this class was how to get used to a new architecture. My quick exposure to MIPS and ARM gave me an idea of what it was like to start my internship at TI the next summer, where I needed to learn the architecture of a product that wasn't even in production yet.

Senior Year (Fall 2019 and Spring 2020)

  • Design and Analysis of Algorithms (CSCE 411)

    This class taught me the fundamental theories behind several famous computer science problems, such as the haulting problem, Fast Fourier Transform, etc. I was reminded of some of the work I did at TI when I saw problems having to do with graph theory and NP-completeness. Here are some problems we looked at in the class:

    • Divide-and-Conquer Algorithms
      • Matrix Multiplication (Strassen's Algorithm)
      • Fast Fourier Transform and the Inverse (for multiplication of polynomials)
    • Greedy Algorithms
      • Minimum Spanning Tree
    • Dynamic Programming
      • Longest Common Subsequence
      • Matrix Chain Multiplication
    • Graph Analysis
      • Depth-First Search and Breadth-First Search
      • Topological Sorting
      • Shortest Path
    • Network Flow Problems and Linear Programming
    • Randomized Algorithms
    • Complexity Theory
      • Turing Machine Theory (through which the concept was first introduced)
      • P, NP, NP-Completeness, etc.
    • Approximation Algorithms and Fixed-Parameter Tracability
    • Undecidability and the Halting Problem
    • Useful for Formal Language Theory and Computability Theory

  • +*Computer Architecture (CSCE 614)

    I took this class to take a deep dive into the world of computer architecture. I learned about the fundamental design ideas behind developing a new architecture, such as ISAs, a few compiler techniques, pipelining and forwarding, virtual memory and virtual machines, memory hierarchies and technologies, Tomasulo's Algorithm, Out-of-order Execution, Speculative Execution, Dynamic Scheduling with Multiple Issue, Branch Prediction, SIMD ISA extensions and vector processors, multiprogramming basics and GPU architectures, Shared-Memory Multiprocessors, Memory Consistency and Coherence Protocols, and Warehouse-Scale Computing.

    This class served as an excellent precursor to the work I will be doing at TI after I graduate. I was able to apply much of what I learned as an intern directly to what we were learning in class, such as SIMD instructions on the TI C6x and C7x and intrinsics. The class also aided me in teaching me some of the fundamental ways to do (and not to do) research. For our class project, we were tasked with developing an innovative new cache replacement policy for a 3-level exclusive cache hierarchy. I produced an IEEE Conference paper describing my technique. I studied a few other techniques to motivate my algorithm before implementing it in the provided simulator. Apparently, the prefetcher was not very good.

  • Distributed Systems (CSCE 438)

    In this class, I started at the socket layer and went up (as opposed to CSCE 463, where I followed the top-down approach). The class began with the basics of time synchronization and coordination of communicating entities. These problems are some of the most difficult systems-level problems to solve. They are hot topics of many of the world's systems conferences, which is why we explored several research and engineering white papers throughout the class, as listed below.

    • The Part-Time Parliament, by Leslie Lamport (The original Paxos paper, as well as the simple version)
    • The Chubby lock service for loosely-coupled distributed systems, by Mike Burrows
    • ZooKeeper: Wait-free coordination for Internet-scale systems, from Yahoo! Grid and Yahoo! Research
    • The Google File System, from Google
    • MapReduce: Simplified Data Processing on Large Clusters, from Jeffrey Dean and Sanjay Ghemawat at Google
    • Bigtable: A Distributed Storage System for Structured Data, from Google
    • Resource Allocation for Energy Efficient k-out-of-n System in Mobile Ad Hoc Networks, a paper our professor contributed to
    • Hadoop MapReduce for Mobile Clouds, a paper our professor contributed to

    In the class, I completed several projects with my partner, where we designed a "Tiny Social Network Service" to tolerate server failures and load balance incoming client connections. This class was one of my first experiences dealing with both distributed processes and failure tolerance.

  • Software Engineering (CSCE 431)

    I was motivated to take this class from both of my previous internsips at Lockheed Martin and Texas Instrumets, and by the time I signed up for the class, I had already locked in my job at TI as a software engineer. The class taught me a number of skills I can use for the rest of my career. My team and I worked for a real client, the Dance Arts Society at Texas A&M University, to provide a real tool to be used in production. Interestingly, our problem mapped to an NP-Hard problem (Travelling Salesman), so our team had to get a little creative with how we used the limited compute/memory available for free for our project.

    The project can be seen here.

    In this class, I learned how to use Ruby on Rails as a MVC Framework for engineering software to meet a client's needs. We learned about the product lifecycle in the Plan-and-document approach and why Agile is often favorable to it. We learned about scrums and teaming, as well as good practices for coding and testing that I feel I can learn in just about any programming situation. The textbook for the class came from here (Engineering Software as a Service).

  • Information Storage and Retrieval (CSCE 470)

    This class taught me a lot about how (mostly) unstructured data is retrieved from the world around us and stored in such a way that provides the system the best performance for queries. The class is strongly motivated by text processing, much like that done by Google, where techniques such as clustering and classification are useful to the users of the system. We learned how to index textual data in structures such as an inverted index and raw term frequency vectors. Below is a summary of some of the topics we covered and practiced:

    • Web Crawlers
    • Inverted Index and Boolean Retrieval
    • Vector Space Model, BM25(F), and Language Models (as well as BIM)
    • Relevance Feedback (pseudo relevance feedback, query expansion, etc.)
    • Evaluation Methods: Recall, Precision, F1, Precision @ K, MAP, MRR, NDCG
    • Classification (Rocchio, Naive Bayes, k Nearest Neighbors) and Learning-to-rank (Machine Learning)
    • Clustering (k-means and HAC)
    • Link Analysis (PageRank and HITS)

    In the class, we also produced a final project showing off the skills we learned in the course. Our team produced MovieMash, which we wrote about in this (surprisingly good) paper. Through this project, I learned about how to use ElasticSearch for data storage and (thus) Kibana for visualization.

    In this final ESET course, we learned about the product lifecycle, as well as other various topics devoted to the business purposes of engineering embedded systems. I learned a great deal about when to patent, how to bring a product to market, and how to manage a team of diverse engineers, among other topics. I worked with a team engineers in the ESET and MXET departments to reverse engineer the WYZE Pan Cam and devise a strategy to bring a new product to market of our own making, as an exercise in development of a new product.
