About Me

My name is Troy Fulton, and I am a Computer Science Major from The Woodlands, Texas, but more importantly, I am the loudest, proudest member of the Fightin' Texas Aggie Class of 2020. Whoop!

tl;dr: As the world turns to embedded technology to solve difficult problems, I seek ways to gain not only the skills to enable such technology but also the knowledge of how the rest of the world can benefit from computational power. I have a long history of sticking my fingers in pies, and now that I am armed with an Aggie engineering degree, I seek every opportunity to develop new skills so that some day, I can positively and professionally contribute to the world around me.

Who am I?

I was born and raised in The Woodlands, Texas, where I lived in an Aggie family until I moved to College Station to start my Engineering Bachelors Degree at Texas A&M University in August 2016. I love traveling, solving puzzles, and exploring the great outdoors. Although I am constantly developing new hobbies, I still like to continue the old ones. Two of my favorite passtimes are playing the trumpet and taking hikes in adventurous places.

Every day is the perfect day to learn something new. I have noticed that when we computer scientists are faced with a difficult problem, we often solve it by first seeking out ways to map those difficult problems to problems we know how to solve. That's why I have made it my mission to solve- not just as many problems as I can- but as many types of problems as I can, even if it means failing the first (few... hundred) tries.

One of the most valuable facets of my Aggie education was the access to endless opportunities to solve any kind of problem I'm willing to put my mind to. For example, classes outside my field of study, such as Atmospheric Science, Anthropology, and Probability, gave me the chance to learn what kind of problems meterologists, paleoanthropologists, and even other engineers solve and how they could benefit from computational power. Honors Programs and experiences like my Peer Teaching Job allowed me to meet distinguished students across the University and learn more about how they view the world.

Of course, solving difficult problems often comes with round after round of what Edison experienced in his first 10,000 light bulb designs. As a consequence of constantly putting myself in situations outside my comfort zone, I expose weaknesses that help me to improve. I am not a perfect programmer- or human being for that matter! Even after playing my instrument for over 11 years, I still walk off the stage of every concert thinking of ways I could make myself a better trumpet player. That's why I do things like spending a weekend developing a novel software tool at TAMUHack and helping pitch my team's project as a product to judges. I can always be faster or better at problem-solving, but as you'll see throughout this portfolio, I strive just as much to learn soft skills.

Where have I been?

I have a long history of changing my mind. As I started high school, I thought I wanted to fuse my artistic and mathematical talents and become an architect. Then, after I place in the top 10 trumpet players in the state of Texas, I was sure I wanted to study music performance. Once I began my college searches, I realized I wanted to pursue something a little more right-brained and began applying to study engineering at schools across the country. For much of my first year at Texas A&M University, I was not entirely sure what I wanted to do with my Aerospace Engineering Degree after I graduated, but I came in ready to soak up as much knowledge as I could to guide my decision. Once I started to see how much I love problem-solving using programming, I found that my passions resided in computer technology.

From then on, I strived more to explore my interests with an open mind, and I'm so glad I did. Ever since I fell in love with programming, I have approached everything with an open mind. For example, although I didn't think I would like studying computer architecture, my computer organization class motivated me to study computer architecture both as a research project and as a minor in embedded systems. Thus, I found that I have a passion for embedded technology and applied to work with Texas Instruments, a world leader in embedded processing. The more I study computer science, the more I realize I don't know about the world and how much better I learn when I admit that I don't and can't know everything.

I've made so many friends and memories at Texas A&M. I met so many astounding students in a myriad of disciplines through the University Honors Program- especially as a freshman living on campus. Throughout my years at Texas A&M, I got the chance to perform all over the world with the Texas A&M University Wind Symphony. As a freshman, I got to participate in a research experience alongside Aerospace Engineers for Aggie Challenge. I also got to join Engineering Honors as a sophomore and participate in research with the department as a senior. I was also lucky enough to spend my summers working at some incredible companies and studying abroad in Peru!

Where am I?

I graduated from Texas A&M University (from home) in May 2020, and I am about to start work at Texas Instruments, Sugar Land in June (from home). I just recently completed the University Research Scholars program, meaning that my undergraduate thesis was approved to be published on the OAKTrust Digital Repository. Now that I have a degree, I'm starting my career at TI in the software engineering rotation program.

Where am I going?

I am learning a lot about TI as I start my job. One thing I have learned is that now that I am out of school, I have a lot more time to myself to explore new interests. I have sketched out several new project ideas I plan on bringing to life! I am also in the process of continuing my research on Active-Routing with HPCL at Texas A&M University for a journal paper.
