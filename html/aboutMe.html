About Me

My name is Troy Fulton, and I am a Computer Science Major from The Woodlands, Texas, but more importantly, I am the loudest, proudest member of the Fightin' Texas Aggie Class of 2020. Whoop!

tl;dr: As the world turns to embedded technology to solve difficult problems, I seek ways to gain not only the skills to enable such technology but also the knowledge of how the rest of the world can benefit from computational power. I have a long history of sticking my fingers in pies, and now that I have gained the focus to pursue an Aggie engineering degree, I seek every opportunity to develop new skills so that some day, I can positively and professionally contribute to the world around me.

Who am I?

I was born and raised in The Woodlands, Texas, where I lived in an Aggie family until I moved to College Station to start my Engineering Bachelors Degree at Texas A&M University in August 2016. I love traveling, solving puzzles, and exploring the great outdoors. Although I am constantly developing new hobbies, I still like to continue the old ones. Two of my favorite passtimes are playing the trumpet and taking hikes in adventurous places.

Every day, technology becomes more integrated with our everyday life ("smart" technology in cars, cell phones, factories,... you name it!). I see endless opportunities in the future for processing power and the rising need for software to help solve the universe's most difficult problems. As a blossoming engineer with aspirations to become a leading embedded software architect, I am always seeking ways to both deepen my understanding of modern computer science research and broaden my understanding of where, exactly, computers fit in this world of endless opportunity.

Every day is the perfect day to learn something new. I have noticed that when we computer scientists are faced with a difficult problem, we often solve it by first seeking out ways to map those difficult problems to problems we know how to solve. That's why I have made it my mission to solve- not just as many problems as I can- but as many types of problems as I can, even if it means failing the first (few... hundred) tries.

One of the most valuable facets of my Aggie education the access to endless opportunities to solve any kind of problem I'm willing to put my mind to. For example, classes outside my field of study, such as Atmospheric Science, Anthropology, and Probability, give me the chance to learn what kind of problems meterologists, paleoanthropologists, and even other engineers solve and how they could benefit from computational power. Honors Programs and experiences like my Peer Teaching Job allow me to meet distinguished students across the University and learn more about how they view the world.

Of course, solving difficult problems often comes with round after round of what Edison experienced in his first 10,000 light bulb designs. As a consequence of constantly putting myself in situations outside my comfort zone, I have exposed weaknesses I wish to improve. I am in no way a perfect programmer- or human being for that matter! Even after playing my instrument for over 11 years, I still walk off the stage of every concert thinking of ways I could make myself a better trumpet player. That's why I do things like spend a weekend developing a novel software tool at TAMUHack and help pitch my team's project as a product to judges in an elevator pitch. I can always be faster or better at problem-solving, but as you'll see throughout this portfolio, I strive to learn soft skills just as much.

Where have I been?

I have a long history of changing my mind. As I started high school, I thought I wanted to fuse my artistic and mathematical talents and become an architect. Then, after I place in the top 10 trumpet players in the state of Texas, I was sure I wanted to study music performance. Once I began my college searches, I realized I wanted to pursue something a little more right-brained and began applying to study engineering at schools across the country. For much of my first year at Texas A&M University, I was not entirely sure what I wanted to do with my Aerospace Engineering Degree after I graduated, but I came in ready to soak up as much knowledge as I could to guide my decision. Once I started to see how much I love problem-solving using programming, I found that my passions resided in computer technology.

From then on, I strived more to explore my interests with an open mind, and I'm so glad I did. Ever since I fell in love with programming, I have approached everything with an open mind. For example, although I didn't think I would like studying computer architecture, my computer organization class motivated me to study computer architecture both as a research project and as a minor in embedded systems. Thus, I found that I have a passion for embedded technology and applied to work with Texas Instruments, a world leader in embedded processing. The more I study computer science, the more I realize I don't know about the world and how much better I learn when I admit that I don't and can't know everything.

Where am I?

Texas A&M University has been an excellent fit for me. For the past three years, I have enjoyed the benefits of the University Honors program, especially as a freshman. I was able to quickly make friends with a community of scholars across a myriad of disciplines whom I may not have gotten the chance to learn from otherwise. I enjoy playing trumpet with the Wind Symphony here, as it has given me an avenue to express my appreciation for music, balance the hemispheres of my brain, and make new friends with others who share my passion for making music. Texas A&M University is a place where curiosity thrives. I am lucky that as long as I remain passionate about learning as much as I can, fellow Aggies will always support my educational efforts.

Currently, I am interning with Texas Instruments in Sugar Land, Texas working on a team that designs compilers for our various products so that customers can continue to write code in high-level languages for any new processor we produce. I am excited to learn about software development as I make more progress in my summer project. I am also preparing to present a project proposal for my research in On-Chip Networks through the Undergraduate Research Scholars Program, as well as preparing to take a trip to Lima, Peru!

Where am I going?

I am striving to complete the Texas A&M University Undergraduate Outcomes as I set out to fulfill the requirements of my degree. I am constantly working to gain depth and breadth in my coursework by taking classes that challenge me to think critically about how I can master the knowledge and apply my knowledge as a professional. Experiences like my internships and research give me the chance to develop my communication skills. I work to practice social and personal responsibility by involving myself in my local community and by working to become a more global citizen. I aim to involve myself in collaborative activities by engaging in various activities, such as those I do through school that prepare me for lifelong learning.

In August, I will be heading to Peru to study Engineering Ethics, and beginning Fall 2019, I will be preparing my thesis in On-Chip networks, which will be published in April 2020- the month before I graduate! I have completed much of my computer science education, so now, I am focusing on applying what I have learned to the DSP product line at TI. I am hopeful that I will get a return offer and continue writing software to improve TI's line of products and contribute to their global footprint.
